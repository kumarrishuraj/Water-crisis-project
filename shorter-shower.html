Take Shorter Showers

Reduce your shower time to five minutes or less to save water and energy. This practice helps conserve 10-15 gallons each time, benefiting both the environment and your utility costs.

Tips for Saving Water in the Shower

1. Use a Shower Timer

Shower timers are simple devices that remind you to finish up before time runs out. You can find ones that suction to the shower wall, and some even have a water-resistant alarm that beeps when your time is almost up. Setting a timer for 4-5 minutes can help you stick to a water-saving schedule without feeling rushed.

2. Try Navy Showers

A technique used by sailors to conserve water, the "Navy shower" involves wetting yourself, turning off the water while you lather up, and then turning it back on briefly to rinse. This method can save significant amounts of water—up to 75%—since you’re only running water for the bare minimum time needed to rinse.

3. Switch to a Bucket-Based Rinse

If you’re open to a minimalist approach, use a small bucket to rinse off soap and shampoo instead of keeping the shower running. You can keep refilling a handheld bucket to rinse thoroughly, yet control exactly how much water you’re using. This method can be surprisingly refreshing, especially in warmer climates.

4. Invest in a Thermostatic Valve

A thermostatic valve allows you to set your ideal temperature and keeps the water steady without having to fiddle with the handles each time. These valves also minimize water wastage while waiting for hot water to come through since they maintain a constant, warm water temperature without the need for pre-rinsing.

5. Catch Cold Water for Reuse

Place a bucket under the showerhead to catch the cold water that flows before the hot water kicks in. This water can be reused for things like watering plants or flushing toilets, which makes it a practical way to avoid waste. A typical shower releases up to 2 gallons of cold water before it warms up, so saving this can make a significant impact.

6. Use Eco-Friendly Shower Products

Eco-friendly soaps and shampoos rinse off more easily and with less water than traditional products. For instance, sulfate-free shampoos tend to lather less and are quicker to rinse out. When used together with reduced shower time, this can also lessen water usage.

Environmental Impact

Adopting water-saving methods in the shower does more than just reduce your water bill—it directly contributes to local water conservation efforts and energy savings. For example, heating water accounts for a substantial portion of household energy usage. Every gallon of hot water saved helps reduce demand on local reservoirs and energy resources, which is essential as many cities face growing populations and potential water scarcity.
